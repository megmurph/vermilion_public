What changed

Sometime in the ~6 months before mid-2026, LinkedIn replaced its old engagement-counting pipeline (separate systems tallying likes, connections, content quality) with a single foundational model that reads posts much closer to how a human editor would. The feed stopped tallying engagement and started interpreting meaning — it now infers what a post is about, who it's useful for, and whether it shows real expertise. Reach fell hard across the board (one widely-cited report: median reach down ~74% YoY), so the 2022-era playbook is now working against you.

Two direct consequences: engagement pods are dead (coordinated like/comment rings no longer move anything), and generic AI-generated "slop" is actively suppressed — LinkedIn is explicitly down-ranking it.

The five rules


Your audience trains the algorithm. The model is doing casting: it looks at who keeps stopping to read you. If marketing directors and founders dwell on your posts, it finds more of them; if it's your mom and three college friends, it serves you to more of them. Get clear on your "Market of 1" (your exact ICP) and write directly to that one person.
The algorithm can smell BS. It rewards lived, specific expertise over polished-but-empty phrasing. Compare: "Content strategy is more important than ever in today's digital landscape" (robotic, generic) vs. "We spent 6 hours rebuilding our content briefing process and cut revision cycles by 42%" (real). The more a post reads like it came from real experience, the better it travels.
Your profile is a ranking signal. Before ranking a post, LinkedIn evaluates the person. A profile that says "CMO" but whose feed is AI tools Monday, marathon training Wednesday, a marketing take every third Tuesday → the model can't tell what you are, so it deprioritizes everything. Own a few clear lanes and stay consistent inside them. (Quick audit: run LinkedIn's AI headline generator — what it suggests is the platform's best guess at who you are. If that makes you say "wait, what?", you have a positioning problem.)
Build for the second read. Likes are now near-worthless ("a nod in the hallway"). The model weights deeper signals: saves carry ~5x the weight of a like, sends/DM-shares signal real value, and dwell time + genuine comments matter. A post with 10 real comments beats 50 drive-by likes. This is why frameworks, checklists, decision trees, benchmark tables, and teardowns are everywhere — they're the formats people save, send to a coworker, and come back to weeks later.
Don't build on rented land. LinkedIn will overhaul this again. Optimize for the goal, not the current ruleset: get your right buyer to find you, trust you, and end up in your world. Track audiences you actually own — DMs from real ICPs, profile visits from target accounts, and newsletter subscribers. "LinkedIn is where they discover you; your newsletter is where you keep them."


How this maps to a launch / funding post


Write the founder post for one specific reader (Rule 1), in plain first-person from real experience, not press-release voice (Rule 2).
Make sure the founder's profile clearly signals their lane before launch day, so the post inherits a strong author signal (Rule 3).
Engineer for saves/sends, not likes: include something reference-worthy — a benchmark, a before/after metric, a short framework, or a teardown of the problem — so people save and DM it (Rule 4).
Capture the reach into something you own: drive profile visits and newsletter signups, not just applause (Rule 5).
Pods and like-for-like asks are wasted effort now — the "favor" you ask friendlies for is a true sentence in their own words (already the guidance in linkedin-launch-playbook.md), which is exactly what the new model rewards.
